How does one describe _The Bends_? Radiohead's sophomore album, released in 1995, is also probably responsible for shaping the wave of Britpop that came out of the late 90s and early 2000s, casting its formidable shadow over bands like Coldplay, Muse, and Travis, who arguably only sound like Radiohead if this is your first time listening to British rock music. Coldplay is warmer, Muse more theatrical, and my apologies to Travis, but I really only know them thanks to countless comparisons to Radiohead. But we aren't here to talk about them. 

The Bends kicks things off (literally, the bass drum on this track set the tone for the entire album, I'd say) with _Planet Telex_ sounding like (as someone on the internet once said) your walk back home from high school, except you're floating instead. This is then followed by the grungy title track ("I wish it was the 60s, I wish I could be happy" sounds like a pop culture summary of Radiohead), the acoustic, closest-to-happy song _High and Dry_ and then the emotionally devastating _Fake Plastic Trees_ (famously referred to as "crybaby music" by Alicia Silverstone's character in the film _Clueless_ ).

I think it is here we can really see Jonny start to shine as a guitarist, similar to the Edge, and in the vein happy guitarist Jonny (Buckland) would come to follow, the very British way of putting a lot of emotion into a guitar solo without overpowering or taking away from anything else in the song. 

_Bones_ is another grungy song (sometimes I must admit, I get it mixed up with the title), and then comes _(Nice Dream)_ which is quintessential Radiohead ("if you think that you're strong enough/ if you think you belong enough") and other whimsically depressing quips. _Just_ is also another track Jonny pushes his guitar to the max on, building off a very satisfying 4 chord riff. The music video is also very memorable, if only because it leaves the viewer in suspense. 

_My Iron Lung_ is another cynical 90s song, that can be applied to a lot of things now, and at any point in time previously as well I suppose. _Bulletproof_ is another whimsical sad acousticky song (they more or less alternate between the two on the album), which is then followed by the closest Radiohead has ever been to sounding like Oasis, _Black Star_. Surprisingly it works very well, even if nobody knows just what exactly the "Black Star" is. 
 
_Sulk_ and _Street Spirit_ close out the album, with the duality of (not necessarily joyful) exuberance and lethargy that pervades the album. _Street Spirit_ has an extremely recognizable guitar riff that serves as the base of the track and carries the album to its rather depressing conclusion. 

Overall, the album sounds exactly like what you'd expect an English rock band to make. It sounds like they've been locked in a shed at the bottom of their garden in a town called Bexley-upon-Thwaite and haven't seen the sun in 27 consecutive days. This is not actually a bad thing, as it somehow lends itself to a spectrum of pretty much universal human emotions (or maybe that's the colonial hangover in me talking). It's also interesting, as Radiohead the band shines through pretty well here, as opposed to their later work, which I would argue is more artsy, but therefore also less human, if that makes any sense.
