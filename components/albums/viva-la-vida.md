Viva la Vida is perhaps considered Coldplay's magnum opus, and with very good reason. In 42 minutes, Coldplay manages to tell us the story of revolutionaries toppling a tyrannical monarchy through stadium-worthy tracks that are still as popular nearly 20 years later. It's rare that an album blends story-telling, instrumentation and for lack of a better word, lore, into a cohesive piece of art, but here is Viva la Vida. 

The opening track of the album, *Life in Technicolor* begins with a slow fade into a sample of Jon Hopkins' Light Through the Veins before breaking into a riff played on a tabla and santoor. The rest of the band gradually joins in, culminating in a joyous "oh oh oh" part at the end, as the band is renowned for. But perhaps the most important function of this track is to set the tone of the album, that wonderfully exuberant, warm feeling that pervades throughout even the most anguished moments of the album. It also serves to showcase how much Coldplay can really achieve, even without Martin's iconic vocals.

The first track with vocals, *Cemeteries of London* sounds unlike anything the band has done before. Eerie and atmospheric, it paints a picture of wanderings among the dead of London, with odd timings and claps pushing the song along. Jonny's guitar work really shines through here, with riffs cutting through the rest of the instruments right before the climatic final verse. 

*Lost!* doesn't really need to add the exclamation point to its title, as the song itself manages to underscore the message it shares with melodramatic pipe organ, and drumming that numerous publications have referred to as "african-inspired".*Lost!* is a defiant anthem, the underdog's explanation for his lack of victory.

"Those who are dead are not dead" is the line Martin opens *42* with, a plaintive piano ballad musing on the meaning of life, complete with somber harmonies and strings. The band build this up gradually into a frenzy of noisy guitars and drums, before running away with the song entirely into a cheery pub singalong. 

Quite possibly the most joyous song ever written, *Lovers in Japan* is a masterpiece of a song. Built around a honkytonk piano riff that Guy, Jonny, and Will spent hours pushing tacks into piano hammers to achieve, the song has incredible rhythm from Guy and Will, with the Buckland throwing in three guitar layers for good measure. This is bridled rage, kept in check by the perpetual joy that Coldplay seem to possess regardless of the circumstance. It is also paired with the wistful *Reign of Love*, in which Chris describes the ideal world with comfort for all, where even the "locusts will lift me up". 

*Yes* is one of the strangest tracks in Coldplay's discography, and that's saying something. Mostly fuelled entirely by an intoxicating blend of strings, percussion, Jonny's guitar layers, and Guy's sublime bass (that punch in the chorus is just insanely satisfying), the song also changes from 4/4 to 3/4 quite a few times. Chris also ditches his usual falsetto for a far lower register, weirdly channeling some form of Muse energy into the song as a whole. The whole thing gets even stranger when halfway through the track, the song ends and *Chinese Sleep Chant* begins, a My Bloody Valentine inspired acid trip, that was supposedly written under hypnosis. 

Possibly the most successful song Coldplay have ever released, *Viva la Vida*, (one of) the title track(s) is a lament evoking imagery of kingdoms and armies and glories lost, over a symphonic crescendo of strings, bells, and kettle drums. Theatrics and instrumentation aside, it is certainly strange that a song sung from the perspective of a dethroned monarch ended up as 2008's Song of the Summer, even going on to win Song of the Year at the following Grammy Awards, topping the Billboard 100 charts, and cementing its position as a crowd-favourite anthem in all their live shows. Accused multiple times of plagiarising various songs, revived by Minecraft, and inspiring various memes, the song is now iconic.

The lead single off the album, *Violet Hill* is that rare thing, a cynical Coldplay song with no real resolution. Jonny features prominently here, I don't think any other song of theirs save *Politik* has him playing so angrily. Raging against all the injustice in the world, with digs at Fox News and false prophets, this is one slow rager of a song. The line "When the future's architectured by a carnival of idiots on show, you better lie low" playing over a montage of politicians dancing is beautifully poignant.

The final single to be released from the album, over a year later, *Strawberry Swing* is the antidote to Violet Hill, sweet and wistful where the latter is angry and caustic. Again built around one of Jonny's guitar riffs, Coldplay uses synths and layers to achieve this sort of hazy effect that's probably the musical equivalent of rose-tinted glasses. Guy probably has a significant role in the creation of this atmosphere. So good even Frank Ocean did a cover of it. 

The other title track, *Death and All His Friends* is one of the sweetest and most Coldplay Coldplay songs out there. Beginning with just Chris and a piano, Jonny comes in with a guitar lick that somehow moves you to tears with a single note, as he is apt to do. But then the whole song changes and becomes another rousing anthem, a spectacular way to close an album of this calibre. Complete with crashing drums, railing against the Title Characters, and wonderfully stacked layers of guitar, piano and bass, it winds down with the same sample we began this whole thing with. 

And here is where we leave the album, lying on our deathbed, looking back at life and what we made of it. Perhaps the world we leave is better than the one we came into. Perhaps our accomplishments have all been eroded even before we have passed on. But no matter. We cannot lie around becoming "a cycle of recycled revenge". We will give into Death and go peacefully. That is, unless we have no other future Prospekts......
