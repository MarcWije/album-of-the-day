Pretty.Odd. is one of the strangest left turns in pop punk. After the success of their debut album, *A Fever You Can't Sweat Out*, Panic! at the Disco decided to spend the money they had made on buying some mushrooms and retreating to a cabin in the mountains to record the follow-up. Unsurprisingly (or not), they were unable to write anything satisfactory, and ended up scrapping most of what they wrote in the cabin (a collection of unreleased songs now known as the legendary *Cricket and Clover*). Upon returning to their hometown of Las Vegas, they began to write songs pretending they were still under the effects of said mushrooms, and the result was the Beatles-esque, psychedelic sunshiney, Pretty. Odd.

From the get go, Pretty. Odd. is vastly different from its predecessor, both in terms of sound and lyrical content. Ditching electric guitars and synths for acoustics and orchestral sections, the album was classified as Baroque Pop thanks to the abundance of strings and trumpets that can be heard on almost every track. Also gone are the angsty, explicit lyrics of *Fever*, replaced by whimsical, daydream-like stories that wouldn't be out of place in a children's book. 

The album opens with Brendon straight up lying to us, singing "You don't have to worry 'cause we're still the same" in *We're So Starving*. This segues straight into what is possibly the most famous track on the album, *Nine in the Afternoon*, a joyfully optimistic celebration of the bizzare (as evident in the gorgeous music video) which ended up becoming a multi-platinum hit. 

*She's a Handsome Woman* carries on with the bizzare lyrics ("film the world before it happens"), but is more straightforward instrumentally, using a standard rock and roll combo of electric guitar, piano, bass and drums. *Do You Know What I'm Seeing?* is a whimsical song, that ponders the weather mostly and has chord changes Bowie would be proud of. The most direct reference to whatever they had in the mountains can be found on *That Green Gentleman (Things Have Changed)*, which is again a track that rather encapsulates the spirit of the album with the lyrics ("Things have changed for me, and that's OK"), while being more of a straightforward song musically. The music video also pays homage to 60s films and children's television, while being one of the best music videos they (or any other pop punk band) has made.

The album then takes an interlude(?) in the form of the ukulele-through-the-telephone *I Have Friends In Holy Spaces*, which is a short, 1-and-a-half minute wistful ramble featuring a couple of trumpets, which then transistions into the most serious (and devastatingly sad, if you love this album) song on the album, *Northen Downpour*. Another straightforward track that relies heavily on an acoustic guitar, the track also has some of the nicest lyrics ("Hey moon, please forget to fall down"). 

*When the Day Met the Night* has beautiful orchestration, telling the story of an anthropomorphic Sun and Moon with vivid lyrical imagery and complexly layered tracks to match.
