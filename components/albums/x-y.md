With two massively successful albums under their belt already, expectations were high for Coldplay’s third album, especially since they had now established their presence as a formidable live act as well. The road to recording _X&Y_, however, was far from smooth, with arguments and delays running rife. Eventually though, all was sorted out and X&Y was released to much fanfare, reaching no. 1 worldwide, and establishing quite a few Coldplay staples. 

Coldplay opens their third album with a pulsing synth and Chris asking "You’re in control, is there anywhere you wanna go?" on _Square One_, possibly a sarcastic reference to the pressure the band was under to succeed commercially. Nevertheless, Will’s drumming kicks in shortly after for a few bars before Guy and Jonny bring the guitars in and the album starts in earnest. With the same introspective, pleading lyrics, yet somehow sounding bigger than ever before, it’s a brilliant starting point for the album, and a poignant reminder that the band is still back to Square One for this album.

_What If_ is a classic Coldplay song, that is largely forgotten to this day unfortunately. Opening with Chris’ vocals and piano chords, planitively questioning life and right and wrong, it soon builds into a chorus that can honestly only be described as strange, in the best way possible. Chris’ falsetto swells with the strings and Jonny’s guitar riff in the background to be vaguely unsettling and despairing, lending further to the whole theme of space and alienation.

_White Shadows_ is apparently Guy Berryman’s favourite song, and that’s understandable because he has such a catchy bassline in this song. Another example of Coldplay blending so well together: Will’s pounding drums here drive the song together with Guy’s offbeat bassline, and it’s topped off by Jonny riffing on top of it. And, naturally, all of it breaks into a chorus with harmonies and a lot of maybes keeping up with the paranoia and uncertainty of the theme. After two verses it finally gives way to a string + church organ section that (plot twist) transitions into the next song.

_Fix You_ is The Ultimate Coldplay song. Originally written after the passing of Gwyneth Paltrow’s father, it has now become a stadium anthem and one of the most instantly recognizable Coldplay songs. It has all the elements of a classic Coldplay song: Chris on organ/piano, eventually adding the other members until the magnificent bridge of the song. I don’t think it’s possible to describe the sonic perfection that this song is; the point Will’s drums kick in is an example of everything a band can possibly be: a group of people working in perfect synchronization to create something utterly beautiful and far beyond their own individual capabilities. 

Jonny Buckland opens _Talk_ with a memorable riff that begins a new Coldplay tradition: sampling songs from other artists. The riff is taken from a motif in Kraftwerk’s 1981 song _Computer Love_. After the break that was _Fix You_, the theme returns to the uncertainty and worry "about the future" as Chris sings. Sonically the song is very much in keeping with the rest of _X&Y_ with loud jarring guitars, synths, and strings. The music video, in which all 4 members of the band (sorry Phil) are eaten by a giant robot in outer space, is also very memorable.

If there was an award for “Best Strings on an X&Y track", it would probably go to the title track. Opening with them, vaguely foreshadowing the incredible _Yes_, they lend a sense of wooziness that is added to by Guy’s bassline and the odd chords of the chorus. The lyrics "You and me are drifting into outer space” feel very fitting with the music. Ending with a falling-off-a-cliff vocal going into another string section makes this sound rather Beatles-esque on the whole.

Any Coldplay fan worth their salt would tell you that Chris Martin is weirdly obsessed with singing about birds, even when the metaphors don’t really fit. Probably the weirdest of those metaphors can be found on _Speed of Sound_, the lead single and also most commercially successful Coldplay track until Viva la Vida’s release. With an iconic piano riff that critics snubbed for being “too similar to _Clocks_", and the crashing drums and crying guitars of an X&Y track, when Chris sings "Birds go flying at the speed of sound, to show you how it all began” it almost seems like a perfectly logical statement to make.

The last song written for the album, Chris wrote the acoustic ballad _A Message_ on a sofa in the middle of the night, based off a 17th century hymn "My song is Love Unknown". Simple, and yet profound, it sort of encapsulates the message (geddit) of Coldplay, "love to the loveless shown". Or else that’s a My Bloody Valentine reference. Either way, it remains a beautiful, soulful pause of reflection in the album.

_Low_ is, quite frankly, the strangest song on an album full of rather strange songs. Guy Berryman once said that they “threw every synth effect they could” onto this track. There’s a strange siren that pervades the whole track, continuing the feeling of unease that the album tries and sort of succeeds in conveying. “All you ever wanted was love, but you never looked hard enough” is rather cynical for a Coldplay song, and even more jarring on the heels of _A Message_. The climax of the song however, where Chris is just singing "I feel low, oh no" is somehow intensely cathartic and beautiful. A song that grows on you like a parasite.

The final single from the album, _The Hardest Part_ is a rather mournful ballad that sums up the uncertainty of X&Y, with lyrics like "And everything I know is wrong, and everything I do, it just comes undone". It makes for a beautiful live performance though, especially later on (as heard on the live album _LeftRightLeftRightLeft_) when combined with _Postcards from Far Away_ from the _Prospekt’s March_ EP.

_Swallowed In the Sea_ is one of the most beautiful Coldplay love songs, and that’s saying something, seeing as we’re talking about Coldplay. “What good is it to live, with nothing left to give? Forget, but not forgive, not loving all you see?” Pure, heartwarming, and genuine, this is simply Coldplay being Coldplay.

The final track on X&Y is so good, Coldplay decided to name their 2006 World Tour _Twisted Logic_. They then subsequently, for reasons no sane person can comprehend, refused to play it on said tour. Ever. It took a group of 13 year olds to finally convince Chris Martin to sing it live, 10 years after its release. And it is thunderously beautiful. With lyrics of dark cynicism “if somebody made it, someone will mess it up” _Twisted Logic_ brings the whole unease and paranoia to a grand, crashing conclusion, in 6/8 time.

_Til Kingdom Come_ wasn’t intended to be a Coldplay song initially. Originally written for Johnny Cash, it was recorded by the band after the singer’s passing and included on X&Y as a hidden track. It’s a rather sweet conclusion to the album for those that were left unsettled by the darkness of Twisted Logic, and it also featured endearingly in the Amazing Spider-Man. This one is just a straightforward sweet love song.
