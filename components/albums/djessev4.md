Djesse Vol. 4 marks the victorious, wild wrap-up of the loftiest musical adventure in the 21st century. For more than six years, Collier has been creating a four-volume cosmos, and Vol. 4 is the point where he completely demolishes the walls.

I have been a fan of Jacob Collier since 2011, the bedroom YouTube era, when he was a teenager harmonizing himself in his mother's music room in London, playing every instrument by himself with only a loop pedal and a mind-boggling ear. I saw him transforming from a cult internet discovery to Quincy Jones signing him, to six-time Grammy winner, to selling out the O2 Arena. And he's 30 years old.

To me, the most extraordinary thing is how he displays music. It is so different from what any other musician does. It is entirely his own language, his very own way of perceiving and listening to the world, which is exactly why he is often the musician's musician. If you ask a non-musician if they know him, you'll probably get a puzzled look. If you ask a professional, watch their expression change.

Think about what people who really know music say about him. Hans Zimmer has said: "There's musicianship and then there's genius, and then way way way above all that, out in the stratosphere, is Jacob Collier." Chris Martin of Coldplay has simply labeled him "the best musician in the world." These are not empty praises. These are icons talking about someone they genuinely can't explain.

From one end of the spectrum 100 000 Voices, the opening song was created using voices of 100,000 crowd members while on the other, my personal choice, Little Blue with Brandi Carlile, presents Collier not only revealing what he can do but also what music can do. The list of guest artists is almost unbelievable: John Legend, Tori Kelly, John Mayer, Lizzy McAlpine, Madison Cunningham, etc. But, what makes the album truly stand out are its more intimate tracks such as Cinnamon Crush, She Put Sunshine, and Wherever I Go.

Some people just hear a technically talented musician without any emotional side. I hear a man wanting to keep the whole of human music in his hands. Vol. 4 is how it appears to be when he eventually lets go.
